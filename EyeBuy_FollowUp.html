01

Operating Agreement

The Operating Agreement (OA) is the private contract between you and Jason that defines how Eye Buy works as a partnership. Unlike the LLC paperwork (which is public), the OA is just between you two. It does not need to be filed with anyone. Get this right before you file anything.

Think of it this way
The OA is your handshake in writing. If something goes wrong between you and Jason six months from now — someone wants out, someone stops contributing, someone gets a job offer — this document is what protects you both. Don't skip it or rush it.

What Must Be In It

  • Ownership split — What percentage does each of you own? 50/50 is common for two co-founders. Write it down explicitly. Example: "Raj Kapoor — 50%, Jason [Last Name] — 50%"
  • Vesting schedule — Protects both of you if one person leaves early. A standard schedule: ownership is earned over 24 months. If Jason leaves after 6 months, he hasn't fully earned his share yet. Recommended: 24-month vest with a 6-month cliff — nothing vests until month 6, then monthly after that
  • Decision-making — Who can make what decisions? Day-to-day decisions (one person can approve). Big decisions like raising money or selling the company (both must agree).
What is a Managing Member?

Managing Member is the LLC equivalent of a CEO or day-to-day operator. LLCs don't have a traditional corporate structure with a president or CEO, so the Operating Agreement is where you define who actually runs things. In EyeBuy's case, that's Raj.

As Managing Member, Raj has the authority to handle operations without needing Jason's sign-off on every decision — filing state paperwork, managing the bank account, making platform decisions, paying routine bills.

Jason is listed as "Member," which means he's a full 50% owner with equal voting rights on all major decisions. He's just not the one handling day-to-day administration. It doesn't make him less of a partner — it clarifies who's responsible for operations.

Think of it this way: you're both co-founders and equal owners, but one of you needs to be the person whose name is on the operating account and whose signature goes on state filings. That's Raj as Managing Member. Jason's power comes through the unanimous consent requirements — nothing major happens without him.

  • Escrow / payment terms — Write in that Eye Buy holds buyer funds for 7 days before releasing to the seller. This protects you legally when you're sitting on customer money. This is important: without it, holding funds could look like commingling money
  • What happens if someone leaves — Can they sell their share to anyone? Or does the remaining partner have the right to buy them out first? (This is called "right of first refusal.")
  • Compensation — Are either of you getting paid right now? Probably not. Write that down. "No salary until the company generates $X in revenue" keeps things clean.
  • IP ownership — The prototype Raj built belongs to Eye Buy LLC, not to Raj personally. Write this in. Especially important given Jason's employment situation — any IP he contributes should be confirmed as his to give
Where to Get It Done
You can use a template from Northwest Registered Agent (free with their registered agent service) or ZenBusiness. Alternatively, a MN or NJ business attorney can draft one for $300–600. Given the employment law complexity with Jason, one attorney review hour is worth it.

Tax & 1099 Clarity

As a marketplace, Eye Buy has a specific tax profile. The good news: Stripe handles most of the hard parts automatically. Here's what you need to understand before you take your first real transaction.

What Eye Buy Pays Tax On

Key Distinction
Eye Buy is not the seller of the equipment. Eye Buy is the platform facilitating the transaction. That means you pay income tax on your 7% fee only — not on the full transaction value. On a $10,000 sale, your taxable revenue is $700, not $10,000.

The 1099-K — Stripe Handles This

Any seller who processes $600 or more through Eye Buy in a calendar year will receive a 1099-K tax form. This is an IRS requirement. The good news: Stripe generates and sends these automatically as part of Stripe Connect. You don't build this, you don't mail anything — Stripe does it.

Tech Stack — Not Shopify

Clearing Up the Confusion
We are not using Shopify. Shopify is built for businesses selling their own products in a single storefront. Eye Buy is a two-sided marketplace — buyers and sellers are both third parties, transactions flow between them, and Eye Buy takes a cut. Shopify does not support this natively. Shopify Hydrogen (their advanced version) was considered but costs $2,300+/month and still isn't designed for marketplaces. It's on the roadmap for much later, if at all.

What "Stripe Atlas" Actually Is

Stripe Atlas is a Stripe product that helps startups form a company — not a payment service. It costs $500 and primarily forms Delaware C-corporations, which are designed for VC-backed startups raising institutional money. That's not Eye Buy right now. We're forming a simple NJ LLC and using Stripe Connect for payments. Don't confuse Stripe Atlas with Stripe Connect — they're completely different products.

Stripe Connect — How It Works

Stripe Connect is the version of Stripe built specifically for marketplaces. Instead of money going from buyer to one seller, Stripe Connect routes money between multiple parties — and takes instructions on how to split it. Here's how it maps to the prototype Raj has already built.

The Money Flow

What Happens on a $10,000 Sale
Buyer pays $10,000 → Stripe holds it for 7 days → after 7 days, Stripe automatically sends $9,300 to the seller's bank account and $700 to Eye Buy's Mercury account. Eye Buy never manually moves money. Stripe does it all via a parameter called application_fee_amount.
